## Research
Through sustained experience in teaching and curriculum development, I have developed a strong interest in computer science education research—specifically in designing curriculum, pedagogical approaches, and educational tools that expand access to computing for nontraditional and underrepresented students while fostering deep conceptual understanding and a genuine sense of belonging.

As artificial intelligence becomes increasingly integrated into programming workflows, I am passionate about researching how foundational computational concepts, such as control flow, abstraction, and programming best practices, can be taught in ways that promote conceptual understanding rather than surface-level tool use. I want students to understand why their code works, regardless of how AI tools are incorporated into their learning process.

Looking forward, my research interests center on curriculum and pedagogical strategies that support nontraditional learners in developing strong computational identities, integrate structured reflection to help students connect day-to-day work with long-term learning goals, and explore accessible, participatory approaches to teaching computing concepts across disciplines and age groups. 

I was recently involved as an undergraduate researcher through UC Berkeley's (Algorithms & Computing for Education (ACE) Lab)[https://acelab.berkeley.edu/]. My role was studying the efficacy of integrating [Human Contexts & Ethics (HCE)](https://cdss.berkeley.edu/dsus/human-contexts-and-ethics) concepts into undergraduate data science curriculum. I hold an interest in finding ways to ensure that students think critically about the implications of the technical work that we do.
